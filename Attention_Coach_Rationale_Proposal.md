# Rationale Proposal: Attention Coach Attention-Control Training Paradigm

## Potential Applications for Anxiety-Related Dysfunctions and Preclinical ADHD Symptoms

---

## Suummary

The Attention Coach paradigm extends the Majority Function Task (Backward masked) (MFT-M) framework into relational stimuli, two-dimensional motion, feature-binding tasks, and cross-modality carrier swaps. This document reviews the empirical evidence supporting its potential as a digital therapeutic intervention for (a) anxiety-related dysfunctions, particularly exam/test anxiety, and (b) preclinical attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) symptoms. The rationale draws on attentional control theory, metacognitive models of emotional disorder, Theory of Visual Attention (TVA) research, hippocampal function research, and the cognitive training literature.

---

## Part A: Anxiety-Related Dysfunctions

### 1. Theoretical Foundation: Attentional Control Theory

Anxiety disorders are the most prevalent class of psychological disorders and a leading cause of disability worldwide (Remes et al., 2016; Wittchen et al., 2011). A core mechanism underlying anxiety-related dysfunction is impaired attentional control. According to Attentional Control Theory (ACT; Eysenck, Derakshan, Santos, & Calvo, 2007), anxiety disrupts the balance between goal-directed and stimulus-driven attentional systems. Specifically, high anxiety increases the influence of the stimulus-driven attentional system while decreasing the efficiency of the goal-directed system, leading to deficits in three executive functions: inhibition, shifting, and updating (Eysenck et al., 2007; Eysenck & Derakshan, 2011).

Empirical support for ACT has been demonstrated across multiple domains. Coombes, Higgins, Gamble, Cauraugh, and Janelle (2009) showed that high trait anxiety was associated with attenuated performance efficiency (slower reaction times) but not performance effectiveness (accuracy) during goal-directed motor tasks, consistent with ACT predictions that anxiety impairs processing efficiency more than performance outcomes. These findings suggest that anxiety creates a "processing tax" even when final performance appears intact - a pattern that is particularly relevant for high-stakes performance situations such as examinations.

The Attention Coach paradigm directly targets the attentional control mechanisms implicated by ACT. By requiring participants to extract directional and relational invariants from rapidly presented multi-element displays (CCC tasks), and subsequently to bind features under time pressure (BSE tasks), the paradigm exercises the very inhibition, shifting, and updating functions that ACT identifies as impaired by anxiety. The adaptive staircase ensures that training occurs at the threshold of each individual's capacity, maximizing the processing demands that ACT predicts will be most sensitive to anxiety-related deficits.

### 1.1 Relational Stimuli and Hippocampal Engagement: A Mechanistic Extension of Attentional Control Theory

While Attentional Control Theory (ACT) traditionally emphasizes prefrontal and anterior cingulate contributions to attentional control, emerging evidence suggests that the hippocampus plays a critical, underappreciated role in regulating attentional processes - particularly when tasks involve relational or spatially structured stimuli. The hippocampus is not merely a memory structure but a dynamic computational hub that supports relational binding, pattern separation, and the integration of self-motion signals, all of which are engaged by the Attention Coach paradigm's relational and optic flow stimuli.

#### 1.1.1 The Hippocampus as a Relational Processor

Two influential theories have competed to explain hippocampal function. The cognitive map theory posits that the hippocampus constructs spatial maps of the environment (O'Keefe & Nadel, 1978), whereas the relational theory argues that the hippocampus supports amodal relational processing across domains (Cohen & Eichenbaum, 1993; Eichenbaum, 2004). A critical fMRI study by Hartley and colleagues directly pitted these theories against each other by comparing spatial navigation through a city with analogous relational navigation through a social network. Hippocampal activation was driven *only* by spatial relational processing, not by matched nonspatial relational demands (Hartley et al., 2005). These findings suggest that the human hippocampus has a *bias toward spatial-relational processing* - precisely the type of processing demanded by Attention Coach's relational frame tasks (e.g., determining inward vs. outward motion relationships, clockwise vs. counterclockwise patterns) and optic flow stimuli.

This spatial-relational bias has direct implications for attention-control training. When participants process relational stimuli - such as determining whether a majority of elements move inward or outward relative to a central point, or whether motion patterns follow a clockwise or counterclockwise trajectory - they engage hippocampal circuits that are specialized for encoding spatial relationships and self-motion cues. Unlike absolute directional judgments (left vs. right), which can be resolved by simple feature detection, relational judgments require the integration of multiple elements into a coherent spatial framework - a computation for which the hippocampus is optimally suited.

#### 1.1.2 Optic Flow and Hippocampal Engagement

The Attention Coach paradigm's use of optic flow stimuli (radial and rotational motion fields) is particularly significant from a hippocampal perspective. Optic flow - the pattern of visual motion generated during self-motion through an environment - provides critical self-motion information that the hippocampal system uses for path integration and spatial updating. Neuroimaging studies have established robust functional connectivity between optic flow-sensitive cortical regions (V3A, V6, hMT+/V5, and the cingulate sulcus visual area, CSv) and the hippocampus during navigation tasks (Sherrill et al., 2013; Kolarik et al., 2019). Specifically, functional connectivity between left CSv and right hippocampus, and between left CSv and right retrosplenial cortex, is positively associated with self-reported navigation ability during visual path integration (Kolarik et al., 2019).

A recent meta-analysis of neuroimaging studies confirmed that the hippocampus is consistently activated during both optic flow processing and spatial navigation, with overlapping activation clusters in the posterior hippocampus (Sulpizio et al., 2024). The hippocampus, together with the retrosplenial cortex and posterior parietal cortex, forms a cooperative network with optic flow regions (V3A, V6, hMT+) to support goal-directed navigation requiring path integration. These functional connections suggest a dynamic interaction between self-motion processing and navigationally responsive systems.

At the cellular level, hippocampal place cells are directly influenced by optic flow signals. Madhav and colleagues (2024) demonstrated that pure optic flow information is sufficient to drive both recalibration of path integration gain and systematic control of place fields in freely moving rats, without requiring landmark-based position signals. This finding establishes that optic flow exerts bottom-up influence on the hippocampal cognitive map and that the brain continuously rebalances idiothetic (self-motion) cues to fine-tune spatial representations. The implication for Attention Coach is profound: optic flow stimuli do not merely engage low-level motion processing but directly activate hippocampal spatial coding mechanisms.

#### 1.1.3 Hippocampal Theta Oscillations and Attentional Control

The hippocampus generates theta (θ) oscillations (4–12 Hz) that are linked to integrative processes critical for higher cognitive functions, including attention, memory encoding, and retrieval (Buzsáki, 2020). Neuronal spiking in widespread brain regions - including somatosensory, entorhinal, and prefrontal cortices - is phase-locked to hippocampal theta oscillations, suggesting that theta serves as a temporal coordination signal for distributed attentional networks (Nuñez et al., 2006; Sirota et al., 2008).

Critically, theta oscillations are modulated by attentional states. During classical conditioning paradigms, hippocampal theta reset follows conditioned stimuli during early training (when orienting and attention are high), but this reset decreases with training as attentional factors stabilise (Buzsáki et al., 1979). More recently, human studies have shown that memory retrieval fluctuates and is time-locked to the phase of ongoing theta oscillations, with phase-locking at the peak of theta preceding eye fixations to retrieved locations (Kerren et al., 2018; Kragel et al., 2020). These findings suggest that the hippocampus coordinates memory-guided attentional orienting via theta-phase gating.

For the Attention Coach paradigm, the engagement of relational and optic flow stimuli may enhance hippocampal theta activity, thereby strengthening the temporal coordination between hippocampal and prefrontal attention networks. Theta-mediated hippocampal-prefrontal interactions are thought to support the binding of distributed representations into coherent cognitive episodes - a process that is directly analogous to the binding demands of the BSE (Bound Signal Extraction) tasks, where participants must integrate direction and colour information under time pressure.

### 1.2 Direct Evidence for MFT-M Training Efficacy 

A recent randomised controlled trial by Zhang and colleagues (2024) provides direct empirical support for the Attention Coach paradigm's foundational training task. The study recruited 84 healthy young adults and randomised them to either seven consecutive days of MFT-M training or a sham control program. Critically, this is the first study to systematically examine training transfer effects from the MFT-M to established measures of executive function, using both behavioural performance and event-related potential (ERP) markers of neural efficiency.

#### 1.2.1 Transfer to Attention Network Function

Participants were assessed on the revised Attention Network Test (ANT-R), which independently measures alerting, orienting, and executive attention (conflict resolution). Compared to the sham control group, the MFT-M training group showed significant improvements on the congruent condition of the Flanker task and the double-congruency condition of Flanker and Location within the ANT-R. These findings demonstrate that MFT-M training transfers to tasks requiring selective attention and conflict resolution - core components of the attention networks that are impaired in both anxiety and ADHD.

The ANT-R is particularly relevant as a transfer measure because its three attention networks (alerting, orienting, executive control) map directly onto the cognitive functions targeted by clinical interventions for anxiety and ADHD. Alerting deficits are associated with ADHD inattention; orienting abnormalities characterise anxiety-related threat bias; and executive attention impairments are found in both conditions. That MFT-M training improved ANT-R performance suggests the paradigm exercises domain-general attention control mechanisms with clinical relevance.

#### 1.2.2 Transfer to Working Memory and Cognitive Flexibility

The training group also showed significant improvements on learning trials in a verbal memory test (AVLT), and ERP analyses revealed neural efficiency changes in both working memory and task-switching paradigms. Specifically, the MFT-M group exhibited a larger P2 amplitude decrease and P3 amplitude increase in the 2-back working memory task, alongside a larger P3 amplitude increase in the task-switching repeat condition. These ERP changes indicate improved neural efficiency in attentional processes underlying working memory maintenance and cognitive flexibility.

The P2 component reflects early attentional selection and stimulus evaluation, while the P3 indexes attentional resource allocation and working memory updating. The pattern observed - reduced P2 (more efficient early processing) coupled with enhanced P3 (better resource allocation)- suggests that MFT-M training optimises the temporal dynamics of attentional control. This neural efficiency profile is precisely what would be predicted to improve performance under the high cognitive demands characteristic of anxiety-provoking situations (e.g., examinations) and the sustained attention requirements of ADHD.

#### 1.2.3 Moderation by Personality: Clinical Implications

A particularly noteworthy finding was that introversion moderated transfer effects. Significant group-by-introversion interactions were observed for post-training 1-back efficiency and task-switching switching costs, with introversion facilitating transfer to working memory while hindering transfer to cognitive flexibility. This moderation effect has direct clinical implications: individuals with higher anxiety traits (who tend toward introversion) may show preferential benefits in working memory domains, while extraverted individuals (potentially including some ADHD presentations) may benefit more from flexibility training.

The Attention Coach paradigm's multi-level structure - progressing from absolute to relational frames, then to binding, and finally to carrier swap - may address this moderation by providing comprehensive training across both working memory and flexibility domains, ensuring that all personality types receive adequate challenge in their relative areas of need.

#### 1.2.4 Baseline Correlations: CCC as a Foundation for Executive Function

At baseline, the Conjunctive Category Control capacity (CCC) estimated from MFT-M performance showed significant correlations with verbal memory performance and task-switching efficiency, and near-significant correlations with inhibition control and ANT-R performance. These correlations corroborate prior research establishing CCC as a common substrate underlying working memory, inhibition, shifting, and fluid intelligence (Chen et al., 2019; He et al., 2019). The baseline CCC in healthy young adults was approximately 3.82 bits per second - substantially higher than the 3.06 bps reported in older adults with mild cognitive impairment, suggesting that CCC is sensitive to both developmental and clinical variation.

For the Attention Coach paradigm, these findings establish that the core MFT-M task is not merely a perceptual exercise but a genuine cognitive control training tool that produces measurable transfer to the executive functions most relevant to anxiety and ADHD intervention. The Zhang et al. (2024) study provides the critical bridge between the basic cognitive science of the MFT-M and its potential clinical application, demonstrating that the task's attention-control demands are sufficiently intensive and domain-general to drive plasticity in the neural systems underlying real-world cognitive performance.

#### 1.2.5 Limitations and Extensions

The Zhang et al. (2024) study was conducted with healthy young adults over a relatively brief training period (seven days). The Attention Coach paradigm extends this foundation in several clinically relevant directions: (a) a longer training duration (20 sessions vs. 7 days) to promote more durable plasticity; (b) the addition of relational and optic flow stimuli to engage hippocampal mechanisms not targeted by the standard arrow-based MFT-M; (c) adaptive QUEST+ staircasing to maintain optimal challenge across the training arc; and (d) the inclusion of feature-binding (BSE) demands to exercise working memory-updating mechanisms beyond those engaged by the binary-choice MFT-M. These extensions are designed to amplify the transfer effects demonstrated by Zhang et al. while targeting the specific cognitive vulnerabilities identified in anxiety and ADHD populations.

### 2. Mechanism of Change: Attention Flexibility

Metacognitive therapy (Wells, 2009) posits that emotional disorders are maintained by the Cognitive Attentional Syndrome (CAS) - a perseverative style of thinking involving worry, rumination, threat monitoring, and unhelpful coping strategies. The Attention Training TechnAttentionue (ATT), a core component of metacognitive therapy, was designed to strengthen attentional control and promote external focus of attention, thereby disrupting the CAS (Wells, 2009).

A randomized controlled trial by Haukaas, Hjeltnes, Birkeland, and Solem (2018) compared ATT with Mindful Self-Compassion (MSC) in university students with symptoms of depression and anxiety. Both interventions produced significant reductions in anxiety and depression symptoms, with effects maintained at 6-month follow-up. Critically, improvement in attention flexibility was the only significant unAttentionue predictor of treatment response across both conditions, suggesting that attentional control is a transdiagnostic mechanism of change (Haukaas et al., 2018). Within-group effect sizes for anxiety measures in ATT studies range from d = 0.32 to d = 0.65, with larger effects for intrusive thoughts (d = 1.06–1.33) and self-focused attention (d = 0.55–1.78) (Knowles, McInnis, Eccles, Eszlari, & Szabo, 2016).

The Attention Coach paradigm operationalises attention flexibility through its multi-level structure. The transition from absolute to relational frames requires attentional shifting; the binding of colour to direction requires divided attention; and the carrier swap from arrows to optic flow requires flexible adaptation to novel stimulus dimensions. Each of these components maps onto the attention flexibility construct that Haukaas et al. (2018) identified as the key predictor of symptom reduction.

### 2.1 Hippocampal Mechanisms in Anxiety Regulation: Why Relational and Optic Flow Stimuli May Enhance Therapeutic Efficacy

#### 2.1.1 The Ventral Hippocampus as an Anxiety Regulator

The hippocampus is structurally and functionally differentiated along its dorsoventral axis. The dorsal hippocampus (dHPC) plays a preferred role in learning and spatial memory, whereas the ventral hippocampus (vHPC) is closely related to emotion and anxiety regulation (Fanselow & Dong, 2010; Bannerman et al., 2004). The vHPC shares bilateral connections with the amygdala and projects to key regulators of affective behaviors, including the hypothalamus, medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC), and nucleus accumbens (Amaral & Witter, 1989; Risold & Swanson, 1996). Lesion and pharmacological studies have established that the vHPC, unlike the dHPC, directly mediates innate fear, anxiety, and stress reactivity (Kjelstrup et al., 2002; Pentkowski et al., 2006).

Recent optogenetic and chemogenetic studies have illuminated the microcircuit mechanisms by which the vHPC regulates anxiety. Within ventral CA1 (vCA1), distinct local inhibitory microcircuits selectively gate the expression of fear and anxiety. Parvalbumin (PV)-positive interneurons exert strong perisomatic inhibition onto pyramidal neurons, regulating their activation during states of uncertainty or potential threat (Hu et al., 2014; Udakis et al., 2020). Optogenetic inhibition of PV interneurons increases pyramidal neuron activity, resulting in heightened anxiety behaviors, such as reduced exploration of anxiogenic environments (Volitaki et al., 2024). Conversely, selective activation of PV+ interneurons in the dentate gyrus produces anxiolytic effects and facilitates fear extinction (Zou et al., 2016).

The vHPC's anxiety-regulating outputs are anatomically segregated: projections to the medial prefrontal cortex, lateral hypothalamus, and lateral septum mediate anxiety and avoidance behaviors, while projections to the basolateral amygdala and central amygdala orchestrate fear learning and expression (Adhikari et al., 2010; Ciocchi et al., 2015; Jimenez et al., 2018). This modular organisation enables the hippocampus to mediate distinct behavioral outcomes depending on contextual demands and outputs.

#### 2.1.2 Hippocampal Regulation of the HPA Axis

The hippocampus is a major regulator of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis - the body's central stress response system. Under normal conditions, hippocampal activity inhibits the HPA axis via negative feedback: the ventral subiculum projects to the paraventricular nucleus (PVN) of the hypothalamus and suppresses corticotropin-releasing hormone (CRH) release, thereby buffering stress responses (Jacobson & Sapolsky, 1991; Herman & Mueller, 2006). Cortisol released by the adrenal glands binds to glucocorticoid receptors in the hippocampus, further activating hippocampal inhibitory control over the HPA axis (Ulrich-Lai & Herman, 2009).

In anxiety disorders, this regulatory loop is disrupted. Chronic stress and elevated cortisol levels damage hippocampal neurons and impair the negative feedback mechanism, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of hypervigilance, continued cortisol elevation, and additional hippocampal damage (Sapolsky, 2000). Hippocampal lesions impair glucocorticoid feedback inhibition, leading to increased stress hormone levels and persistent HPA axis activation - a hallmark of anxiety and depression (Dedovic et al., 2009). The ventral CA1 projections to the lateral hypothalamus have been shown to regulate autonomic nervous system activity and stress hormone release, with activation of this pathway increasing anxiety and avoidance behavior (Jimenez et al., 2018).

#### 2.1.3 Why Relational and Optic Flow Stimuli May Be Therapeutically Advantageous

The Attention Coach paradigm's progression from absolute to relational stimuli, and ultimately to optic flow, may confer therapeutic advantages beyond those of simple feature-based attention training for three hippocampal-related reasons:

**First, relational stimuli engage the dorsal hippocampus's spatial-relational processing mechanisms.** As established by Hartley et al. (2005), the hippocampus is preferentially activated by spatial-relational processing. By requiring participants to encode and manipulate relational frameworks (e.g., "inward vs. outward" or "clockwise vs. counterclockwise"), Attention Coach exercises hippocampal circuits that may be underactivated in anxiety disorders due to chronic stress-induced hippocampal dysfunction. Restoring activity in these circuits through targeted cognitive training could, in principle, strengthen the hippocampal regulatory capacity over the HPA axis.

**Second, optic flow stimuli activate the hippocampal self-motion integration network.** The functional connectivity between optic flow regions and the hippocampus (Kolarik et al., 2019; Sherrill et al., 2013) suggests that optic flow processing is not merely a visual task but a hippocampally-mediated spatial computation. By training participants to extract invariant directional information from optic flow fields, Attention Coach may strengthen the integrity of hippocampal-optic flow networks that support spatial self-monitoring - a capacity that may be disrupted in anxiety, where individuals lose the ability to "navigate" their internal and external environments effectively.

**Third, the carrier swap (arrows → optic flow) may promote hippocampal pattern separation.** Pattern separation - the process by which similar inputs are transformed into less similar, non-overlapping representations - is a core hippocampal computation performed primarily by dentate gyrus granule cells (Yassa & Stark, 2011). The carrier swap requires participants to extract the same relational invariants (e.g., majority direction) from fundamentally different surface features (arrows vs. optic flow), a demand that closely parallels the computational requirements of pattern separation: distinguishing similar but distinct representations. Impaired pattern separation has been linked to anxiety disorders, where threat-related representations generalise excessively across contexts (Kheirbek et al., 2012). Training hippocampal pattern separation through relational stimulus variation may therefore reduce threat overgeneralisation - a core mechanism of anxiety maintenance.

### 3. Specific Application: Exam and Test Anxiety

Test anxiety represents a particularly relevant target for attention-control training because it directly impairs performance in evaluative situations through the same mechanisms described by ACT. Fergus and Limbers (2019) conducted a controlled pilot study examining group-format delivery of ATT among eighth-grade students. Students receiving five sessions of ATT reported significantly less post-intervention test anxiety than a music-listening control group, with effects maintained at 3-week follow-up. The effects were strongest for students with heightened baseline metacognitive beliefs about worry, suggesting that attention training is particularly beneficial for those whose anxiety is cognitively mediated (Fergus & Limbers, 2019).

Working memory training has also shown promise for reducing test anxiety. A recent study (2024) found that 10 days of adaptive dual n-back working memory training significantly lowered test anxiety levels in adolescents, although attentional control measures did not show differential improvement between groups. The authors attributed the anxiety reduction to enhanced emotional regulation capacity, which is consistent with the broader literature linking working memory training to improved affective control (Sari & Koster, 2018; Zhao & Dang, 2020).

The Attention Coach paradigm extends beyond these approaches by integrating adaptive attention-control training with a far-transfer design. Unlike ATT, which trains attention flexibility through auditory exercises, or working memory training, which targets storage and manipulation, Attention Coach trains the extraction of invariant rules across changing surface features. The carrier swap (arrows → optic flow) ensures that what is learned is not tied to specific visual features but to the underlying attentional control processes. This aligns with Barnett and Ceci's (2002) taxonomy of far transfer, which emphasises that transfer is more likely when training targets general principles rather than specific procedures.

### 4. TVA-Based Assessment and Anxiety

The Theory of Visual Attention (TVA; Bundesen, 1990) provides a mathematical framework for decomposing visual attention into separable parameters: processing speed (C), visual short-term memory capacity (K), perceptual threshold (t₀), and top-down selectivity (α). TVA-based assessment has been used in approximately 30 clinical studies investigating attentional deficits across neurological and psychiatric conditions (Finke et al., 2017).

Although direct TVA studies of anxiety are limited, the framework is highly relevant. Anxiety is known to impair processing speed and increase distractibility - precisely the parameters measured by TVA. McAvinue et al. (2015) demonstrated that TVA parameters can distinguish clinical populations with attentional deficits, and the processing speed parameter (C) has been shown to be particularly sensitive to conditions involving impaired attentional control. The Attention Coach paradigm uses TVA-based psychometric functions to estimate capacity (C) in bits per second, providing a theoretically grounded metric that is independent of motor speed and response bias (Bundesen & Habekost, 2008).

By tracking C across the 20-session progression, the Attention Coach system can detect whether training produces genuine increases in processing capacity (as opposed to strategy learning or speed-accuracy trade-offs). This is critical for establishing that the intervention targets the underlying attentional mechanisms implicated in anxiety, rather than merely improving task-specific performance.

## Part B: Preclinical ADHD Symptoms

### 1. TVA Evidence for Attention Deficits in ADHD

Children and adolescents with ADHD show well-documented deficits in attentional control. McAvinue, Robertson, Caldwell, and Bunting (2015) used TVA-based assessment to examine visual attention in children with ADHD aged 9–13. Compared to typically developing controls, children with ADHD showed significantly impaired sustained attention and visual processing speed (C parameter), but intact attentional selectivity, perceptual threshold, and visual short-term memory capacity. These findings suggest that ADHD involves a specific deficit in the speed of attentional processing rather than a global attentional impairment - a profile that is directly addressable through adaptive training targeting processing speed (McAvinue et al., 2015).

The clinical TVA literature has demonstrated the sensitivity of these parameters across neurodevelopmental conditions (Finke et al., 2017). The Attention Coach paradigm's use of TVA-based capacity estimation (C in bits/sec) means that it is measuring the same construct that McAvinue et al. (2015) identified as impaired in ADHD. This provides a direct mechanistic link between the training task and the known cognitive deficit.

### 1.1 Hippocampal Contributions to Attention Deficits in ADHD: Beyond TVA Parameters

#### 1.1.1 Hippocampal Dysfunction in ADHD

While TVA-based research has established that ADHD involves specific deficits in processing speed (C) and sustained attention, converging evidence from animal models, neuroimaging, and neuropsychological studies suggests that hippocampal dysfunction also contributes to the attentional profile of ADHD. The hippocampus is densely interconnected with prefrontal cortical regions that support executive functions, and hippocampal-prefrontal interactions are critical for goal-directed behavior, working memory, and cognitive flexibility - domains consistently impaired in ADHD.

Rodent models of ADHD have demonstrated hippocampal abnormalities in spontaneously hypertensive rats (SHR), a widely used animal model of ADHD. These animals show altered hippocampal long-term potentiation, impaired spatial memory, and disrupted theta oscillations (Berger & Sagvolden, 1998; Giesbrecht et al., 2010). In humans, structural MRI studies have reported reduced hippocampal volume in children and adolescents with ADHD, with volume reductions correlating with symptom severity and executive function deficits (Qiu et al., 2009; Plessen et al., 2006). Functional neuroimaging studies have further shown abnormal hippocampal activation during memory and attention tasks in ADHD, suggesting that hippocampal contributions to cognitive control are compromised (Skudlarski et al., 2008).

#### 1.1.2 Theta Oscillations, Hippocampal-Prefrontal Coupling, and Attention

Hippocampal theta oscillations are critical for coordinating neural activity across distributed brain networks during attention-demanding tasks. In rodents, hippocampal-prefrontal theta coherence increases during working memory and attention tasks, and this coherence is disrupted in animal models of ADHD (Jones & Wilson, 2005; Sigurdsson et al., 2010). Theta-mediated synchronisation is thought to facilitate the transfer of information between the hippocampus and prefrontal cortex, enabling the integration of past experiences with current goals - a process essential for adaptive attentional control.

In humans, hippocampal theta activity is modulated by attentional demands during both spatial and nonspatial tasks. Theta power increases during orienting to novel stimuli, during memory encoding, and during the maintenance of information in working memory (Buzsáki, 2020). Given that ADHD is characterised by impaired orienting, reduced sustained attention, and working memory deficits, the restoration of theta-mediated hippocampal-prefrontal coordination may represent a mechanistic target for cognitive training.

#### 1.1.3 Why Relational and Optic Flow Stimuli May Be Particularly Beneficial for ADHD

The Attention Coach paradigm's emphasis on relational and optic flow stimuli may be especially well-suited to address hippocampal dysfunction in ADHD for several reasons:

**Relational processing demands engage hippocampal-prefrontal circuits.** The transition from absolute (left/right) to relational (inward/outward, clockwise/counterclockwise) frames requires participants to construct and manipulate cognitive maps of stimulus relationships - a function that depends on intact hippocampal-prefrontal interactions. By systematically training these interactions, Attention Coach may strengthen the very circuits that are dysfunctional in ADHD.

**Optic flow stimuli provide naturalistic self-motion signals.** Unlike static arrow stimuli, optic flow mimics the visual input experienced during self-motion through an environment. The hippocampal place cell system is optimised to process such self-motion cues, using them to update spatial representations via path integration (Madhav et al., 2024). For individuals with ADHD, who often show deficits in spatial working memory and navigation (Bullens et al., 2010), training with optic flow stimuli may provide a more ecologically valid and engaging form of attention exercise that recruits hippocampal mechanisms more robustly than static displays.

**The carrier swap promotes cognitive flexibility via hippocampal remapping.** Hippocampal place cells exhibit "remapping" - the reorganisation of spatial firing patterns when environmental features change - while grid cells in the entorhinal cortex realign their firing orientations (Fyhn et al., 2007; Hafting et al., 2005). The carrier swap from arrows to optic flow is analogous to an environmental change that requires hippocampal remapping: the same underlying relational rules must be extracted from a fundamentally different sensory input. This demand for representational flexibility may specifically exercise hippocampal circuits that support cognitive flexibility, a core deficit in ADHD.

### 2. Attention Training for ADHD: Established Near-Transfer Effects

Several structured attention training programs have demonstrated efficacy for improving attentional functions in children with ADHD. Tamm, Nakonezny, and Hughes (2014) conducted a randomised controlled trial of the Pay Attention! program with 105 children aged 7–15. Compared to a waitlist control, the attention training group showed significant improvements in sustained, selective, and alternating attention, as measured by the Test of Everyday Attention for Children (TEA-Ch). Parent and clinician ratings of inattention and hyperactivity also improved significantly, although teacher ratings did not (Tamm et al., 2014).

Tucha, Tucha, Kaumann, Koerts, and Lange (2011) evaluated the AixTent attention training program with 32 children with ADHD. After eight 60-minute sessions over four weeks, the attention training group demonstrated significant improvements in vigilance, divided attention, and alternating flexibility, as measured by the Test Battery for Attentional Performance (TAP). A visual perception training control group did not show comparable improvements, suggesting that the effects were specific to attention training rather than general cognitive engagement (Tucha et al., 2011).

Semrud-Clikeman, Nielsen, Clinton, Sylvester, and Parle (1999) found that Attention Process Training (36 sessions over 18 weeks) normalised visual attention performance in children with attentional difficulties, bringing them to levels comparable to typically developing peers. These studies establish that attention training can produce meaningful near-transfer effects in ADHD populations, particularly for laboratory measures of attention.

### 3. Multiple Object Tracking and ADHD

Multiple Object Tracking (MOT) paradigms share conceptual and mechanistic overlap with the Attention Coach MFT-M framework. Trick, Jaspers-Fayer, and Sethi (2005) found that children with ADHD showed reduced MOT capacity compared to typically developing peers, tracking fewer objects successfully. Importantly, MOT capacity in children with ADHD improved significantly with targeted training, suggesting that dynamic attention tasks are both sensitive to ADHD-related deficits and amenable to remediation (Trick et al., 2005).

More recently, Tullo, Bertone, and colleagues (2018) adapted a standard MOT protocol for students with autism spectrum disorder and ADHD, reducing the number of targets to accommodate developmental differences. After 15 sessions over five weeks, the intervention group showed a 41% improvement in speed thresholds (p < 0.001), with gains observed on near-transfer measures of executive function including cognitive flexibility (Tullo et al., 2018).

NeuroTracker studies have demonstrated the feasibility of MOT-based training in classroom settings for adolescents with neurodevelopmental disorders, including ADHD. One feasibility study reported 100% completion rates among 26 adolescents with extremely low Attention, 15% of whom had ADHD diagnoses (Parsons et al., 2016). These findings suggest that adaptive MOT-style training is accessible and engaging for the target population.

### 4. Cognitive Training Meta-Analyses: The Far-Transfer Challenge

The cognitive training literature for ADHD has produced mixed results, particularly regarding far transfer to untrained domains. Melby-Lervåg and Hulme (2013) conducted a meta-analysis of working memory training studies and concluded that while near-transfer effects to working memory tasks were reliable, there was no convincing evidence of far transfer to nonverbal ability, verbal ability, word decoding, or arithmetic. Cortese et al. (2015) reached similar conclusions in a meta-analysis of cognitive training for ADHD, finding limited support for far transfer to academic performance or behavioral outcomes.

However, these null findings may reflect methodological limitations rather than theoretical impossibility. van der Donk et al. (2013) noted that many studies lacked adequate control conditions, used insufficient training durations, or failed to target the specific cognitive mechanisms underlying ADHD symptoms. Rutledge et al. (2024) argued that future cognitive training research should borrow concepts from precision medicine, tailoring interventions to individual cognitive profiles and using more sensitive transfer measures.

The Attention Coach paradigm addresses several of these limitations. First, the carrier-swap design (arrows → optic flow) implements a principled far-transfer test by changing surface features while preserving the underlying attentional control demands. Second, the 20-session longitudinal design with learning-curve gates ensures that training continues until genuine skill acquisition is demonstrated, rather than imposing a fixed duration. Third, the TVA-based capacity metric (C in bits/sec) provides a theoretically grounded, process-pure measure of attentional control that is independent of task-specific strategies.

### 5. Why Attention Coach May Succeed Where Prior Cognitive Training Has Struggled

Barnett and Ceci (2002) proposed a taxonomy for far transfer identifying nine dimensions along which training and transfer contexts can vary. They argued that successful far transfer is more likely when training targets general principles or heuristics rather than specific procedures, and when the training context varies along multiple dimensions (knowledge domain, physical context, temporal context, functional context, social context, and modality). The Attention Coach paradigm implements these principles through:

1. **Principle-based training**: Participants learn to extract invariant relational rules (e.g., "majority direction") rather than memorising specific stimulus-response mappings.
2. **Multi-dimensional variation**: The carrier swap changes the visual modality (arrows → optic flow), spatial reference frame (absolute → relational), and feature complexity (binary → conjunction), while preserving the core attentional control demands.
3. **Adaptive difficulty**: The QUEST+ staircase maintains training at the individualised threshold where attentional control is maximally challenged, promoting effortful processing that is necessary for neuroplastic change (Klingberg, 2010).
4. **Sufficient dosage**: The 20-session, 1,600-trial design provides substantially more training than many prior studies, which often used 10–15 sessions (Melby-Lervåg & Hulme, 2013).

The hierarchical Bayesian updating of capacity estimates also addresses a key limitation of prior research: the inability to detect small but meaningful changes in underlying capacity. By borrowing strength across sessions and using precision-weighted updating, the Attention Coach system can detect capacity improvements earlier and more reliably than single-session assessments.

### 5.1 Hippocampal Engagement as a Far-Transfer Mechanism

#### 5.1.1 The Hippocampus as a Hub for Far Transfer

Traditional cognitive training research has focused on near-transfer effects (improvements on structurally similar tasks) and has struggled to demonstrate reliable far transfer (improvements on dissimilar, ecologically meaningful outcomes). One explanation for this limitation is that many training paradigms target isolated cortical regions (e.g., dorsolateral prefrontal cortex for working memory) without engaging the broader network interactions that underlie real-world cognitive flexibility.

The hippocampus occupies a unAttentionue position in the brain's cognitive architecture that makes it a potential hub for far transfer. As a convergence zone for information from multiple sensory modalities, the hippocampus integrates inputs from visual, auditory, somatosensory, and olfactory cortices via the entorhinal cortex (Eichenbaum et al., 2007). Its outputs project to prefrontal, parietal, and limbic regions that support executive function, attention, and emotional regulation. This anatomical position enables the hippocampus to serve as a "relational binder" that links disparate cognitive operations into coherent episodes - precisely the type of integrative function that far transfer requires.

#### 5.1.2 Pattern Separation, Cognitive Flexibility, and Generalisation

Pattern separation in the dentate gyrus is essential for discriminating similar experiences and preventing interference between overlapping memories. This computational process has direct relevance to far transfer: to apply learned skills in novel contexts, the brain must distinguish the current situation from previously encountered situations while recognising the underlying structural similarities. Pattern separation enables this discrimination, while pattern completion in CA3 supports the retrieval of appropriate schemas when partial cues are present (Yassa & Stark, 2011; Rolls, 2013).

The Attention Coach paradigm's relational and carrier-swap design directly exercises pattern separation and completion mechanisms. When participants encounter optic flow stimuli after training with arrows, they must discriminate the new carrier (optic flow) from the old (arrows) while completing the relational schema ("extract majority direction") from partial cues. This dual demand - separating surface features while completing relational structures - mirrors the computational requirements of far transfer in naturalistic settings.

#### 5.1.3 Hippocampal Neurogenesis and Training-Induced Plasticity

Adult hippocampal neurogenesis - the birth of new neurons in the dentate gyrus - has been linked to pattern separation, cognitive flexibility, and stress resilience (Gage & Kempermann, 2018). Neurogenesis is enhanced by cognitive engagement, physical exercise, and environmental enrichment, and is suppressed by chronic stress and aging. Importantly, neurogenesis is required for the spontaneous recovery from stress-induced anxiety and cognitive flexibility impairments (Anacker & Hen, 2017).

Cognitive training paradigms that engage hippocampal relational processing may promote neurogenesis-induced plasticity. Memory training studies have demonstrated that intensive episodic memory training can increase hippocampal volume in both young and older adults, with effects persisting for months (Engvig et al., 2014). While the specific effects of attention-control training on neurogenesis have not been directly tested, the engagement of hippocampal pattern separation and relational binding mechanisms by Attention Coach's relational and optic flow stimuli provides a plausible pathway for training-induced hippocampal plasticity.

#### 5.1.4 Implications for Far Transfer to Anxiety and ADHD Outcomes

If the hippocampus serves as a hub for far transfer, then training paradigms that robustly engage hippocampal mechanisms should produce broader transfer than those that do not. For anxiety, hippocampal engagement may facilitate transfer to real-world situations by strengthening the neural mechanisms that discriminate safe from threatening contexts (pattern separation), regulate the HPA axis (ventral hippocampal outputs), and coordinate attentional resources under stress (theta-mediated prefrontal coupling). For ADHD, hippocampal engagement may support transfer to academic and organisational skills by enhancing the relational binding of task-relevant information, the cognitive flexibility to switch between strategies, and the spatial working memory needed for planning and sequencing.

## Part C: Transdiagnostic Considerations

### 1. Attention Control as a Shared Mechanism

Both anxiety disorders and ADHD involve deficits in attentional control, albeit through different pathways. In anxiety, attentional control is disrupted by threat-related bias and worry-driven self-focus (Eysenck et al., 2007; Wells, 2009). In ADHD, attentional control deficits are primary and neurodevelopmental, affecting sustained attention, inhibition, and working memory (Barkley, 1997; McAvinue et al., 2015). However, the downstream consequences overlap: both conditions are associated with impaired academic performance, reduced processing efficiency, and difficulty maintaining goal-directed behavior in the face of distraction.

The Attention Coach paradigm targets attentional control at a level of abstraction that is relevant to both conditions. By training the extraction of invariant rules across varying surface features, it strengthens the goal-directed attentional system that ACT identifies as impaired by anxiety, while simultaneously exercising the processing speed and selective attention mechanisms that TVA research has shown to be deficient in ADHD. The transdiagnostic relevance of attentional control is further supported by the finding that attention flexibility predicts treatment response across both anxiety and depression (Haukaas et al., 2018).

### 1.1 The Hippocampus as a Transdiagnostic Bridge Between Anxiety and ADHD

#### 1.1.1 Shared Hippocampal Vulnerability

Both anxiety disorders and ADHD are associated with hippocampal abnormalities, albeit through different etiological pathways. In anxiety, chronic stress and elevated glucocorticoids damage hippocampal structure and function, impairing the negative feedback regulation of the HPA axis and reducing neurogenesis (McEwen, 2007). In ADHD, neurodevelopmental factors alter hippocampal volume, connectivity, and theta oscillations, contributing to deficits in spatial memory, working memory, and cognitive flexibility (Plessen et al., 2006; Skudlarski et al., 2008). Despite these different origins, the convergent hippocampal dysfunction in both conditions suggests that interventions targeting hippocampal mechanisms may have transdiagnostic efficacy.

#### 1.1.2 Hippocampal-PFC Coupling as a Common Therapeutic Target

The hippocampus and prefrontal cortex are bidirectionally connected via multiple pathways, including the uncinate fasciculus, fornix, and indirect routes through the thalamus and entorhinal cortex. Hippocampal-prefrontal functional connectivity is critical for integrating past experiences with current goals, a process that is impaired in both anxiety (where threat memories intrude on present-moment processing) and ADHD (where goal maintenance is disrupted by distractibility).

Relational and optic flow stimuli may strengthen hippocampal-prefrontal coupling by requiring the integration of spatial-relational information with attentional control demands. When participants process optic flow to extract directional invariants, they must coordinate hippocampal self-motion processing with prefrontal goal-directed attention - a coordination that depends on intact hippocampal-prefrontal communication. Repeated practice of this coordination may, through Hebbian plasticity, strengthen the structural and functional connectivity of the hippocampal-prefrontal pathway.

#### 1.1.3 Theta-Mediated Integration as a Transdiagnostic Mechanism

Hippocampal theta oscillations provide a temporal framework for coordinating activity across distributed brain networks. In both anxiety and ADHD, theta abnormalities have been reported: anxiety is associated with elevated theta in the ventral hippocampus and amygdala (reflecting hypervigilance), while ADHD is associated with reduced theta coherence between hippocampus and prefrontal cortex (reflecting impaired coordination). Training with relational and optic flow stimuli may normalise theta activity in both conditions by providing structured, adaptive demands that entrain theta-mediated coordination without overwhelming the system.

The adaptive QUEST+ staircase is particularly relevant here: by maintaining task difficulty at the individualised threshold of capacity, the Attention Coach paradigm ensures that hippocampal-prefrontal coordination is challenged sufficiently to drive plasticity but not so severely as to induce stress-related theta disruption. This "optimal challenge" principle aligns with the broader literature on neuroplasticity, which suggests that learning occurs most efficiently when tasks are demanding but achievable (Klingberg, 2010).

### 2. The Role of Adaptive Training

Traditional cognitive training programs often use fixed difficulty levels, which may result in either boredom (if too easy) or frustration (if too hard), both of which reduce engagement and learning. The Attention Coach paradigm uses a QUEST+ adaptive staircase that selects exposure times based on expected information gain, ensuring that each trial is optimally challenging. This approach has been shown to triple effective statistical power compared to fixed-exposure designs (Watson & Pelli, 1983), meaning that the 80 trials per session in Attention Coach provide equivalent information to approximately 240 fixed trials.

Adaptive training is particularly important for clinical populations, who may show greater variability in baseline capacity. Children with ADHD, for example, often exhibit high intra-individual variability in response times (Kofler et al., 2013). An adaptive system that adjusts to moment-to-moment performance can accommodate this variability while maintaining optimal challenge levels, potentially improving both engagement and efficacy.

### 3. Gamification and Engagement

A critical challenge for cognitive training interventions is participant adherence. The Attention Coach paradigm incorporates several features designed to enhance engagement: (a) a 20-session narrative arc with clear progression phases, (b) a "carrier swap" event at session 11 that introduces novel challenge, (c) real-time feedback and skill scoring, and (d) a mixed-mode final phase that tests mastery under unpredictable conditions. These design elements align with evidence that game-like features can improve adherence to cognitive training (Katz et al., 2014), particularly among adolescents who may be the primary users for both anxiety and ADHD indications.

## Conclusion

The Attention Coach paradigm is positioned at the intersection of several well-established research traditions: attentional control theory for anxiety, TVA-based assessment for ADHD, metacognitive therapy for emotional disorders, and adaptive cognitive training for neurodevelopmental conditions. While the evidence base for digital therapeutics in these domains remains mixed, the Attention Coach design addresses key methodological limitations that have plagued prior research: insufficient training duration, lack of principled far-transfer tests, fixed (non-adaptive) difficulty, and reliance on task-specific rather than process-pure outcome measures.

The rationale for clinical development rests on four pillars:

1. **Mechanistic specificity**: The paradigm targets attentional control processes that are causally implicated in both anxiety and ADHD, with TVA-based metrics that can verify mechanism-level change.

2. **Far-transfer design**: The carrier-swap structure implements a controlled test of transfer across stimulus dimensions, addressing the "surface strategy" critique that has undermined prior cognitive training claims.

3. **Adaptive precision**: The QUEST+ staircase and hierarchical Bayesian updating ensure that training is individually optimised and that small but meaningful improvements in underlying capacity can be detected.

4. **Hippocampal engagement**: The progression from absolute to relational stimuli, and ultimately to optic flow carriers, is not merely a surface-variation strategy but a principled recruitment of hippocampal mechanisms that are directly implicated in both anxiety regulation and ADHD pathophysiology. Relational stimuli preferentially engage the hippocampus's spatial-relational processing bias (Hartley et al., 2005); optic flow activates hippocampal place cells and self-motion integration networks via functional connectivity with optic flow regions (Kolarik et al., 2019; Madhav et al., 2024); and the carrier swap exercises hippocampal pattern separation and remapping mechanisms that are computationally homologous to far-transfer requirements (Yassa & Stark, 2011). The ventral hippocampus's direct regulation of the HPA axis (Jacobson & Sapolsky, 1991) and its microcircuit control of anxiety behaviors (Kheirbek et al., 2012; Volitaki et al., 2024) provide a neurobiological basis for why hippocampally-engaging training may be particularly efficacious for anxiety. Conversely, hippocampal-prefrontal theta coherence deficits in ADHD (Jones & Wilson, 2005) and hippocampal volume reductions correlating with symptom severity (Plessen et al., 2006) suggest that restoring hippocampal function through relational and optic flow training may address a previously under-targeted component of the ADHD cognitive profile. By embedding hippocampal engagement within an adaptive attention-control framework, Attention Coach targets a convergence point between the attentional and memory systems that prior cognitive training paradigms have largely neglected.

Future research should prioritise randomised controlled trials comparing Attention Coach to active control conditions with pre-registered hypotheses, blinded assessors, and clinically relevant far-transfer outcomes including academic performance, self-reported anxiety/ADHD symptoms, HPA axis biomarkers, and functional impairment measures. Neuroimaging substudies examining hippocampal activation, theta coherence, and structural changes would further elucidate the mechanisms by which relational and optic flow stimuli contribute to therapeutic efficacy."""
